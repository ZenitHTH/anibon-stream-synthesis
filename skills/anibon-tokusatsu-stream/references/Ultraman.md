# Ultraman

## Franchise History
Created by Eiji Tsuburaya (the "Father of Tokusatsu" known for his Godzilla VFX work), the franchise began with Tsuburaya Productions' *Ultra Q* (1966) and the colored *Ultraman* (1966). It pioneered the "Kyodai Hero" (Giant Hero) genre, where heroes transform into towering giants to fight Kaiju. Eiji Tsuburaya's vision was to create stories of magic, hope, and friendship for children. It has evolved through the Showa, Heisei, and New Generation eras, becoming a massive cultural pillar in Japan.

## Key Terms
- **Tiga, Dyna, Gaia**: Classic Heisei trilogy; nostalgia heavy in Thai fandom.
- **Ultraman Nexus**: Dark, serialized story — cult favorite.
- **Ultraman Trigger**: Tiga successor; 2021.
- **Ultraman Blazar (2023)**: อุลตร้าแมนเบลซาร์ — features primitive theme and organic sound effects.
- **Ultraman Arc (2024)**: อุลตร้าแมนอาร์ค — features imagination theme and armor changes.
- **Ultraman Teo (2026)**: อุลตร้าแมนทีโอ — 60th-anniversary milestone series premiering in July 2026 (often confused with Arc in mid-2026 discussion streams).
- **Ultra Galaxy Fight**: Cross-franchise OTT series by Koichi Sakamoto.
- **Tsuburaya Productions**: ซึบุรายะ โปรดักชันส์ — creator of Ultraman, often discussed regarding its corporate struggles and parent company Fields.
- **Kaiju**: Monster/villain (怪獣) — always use the Japanese term.

## Tsuburaya vs. Chaiyo Productions (Sompote Sands) Lawsuit
A decades-long international legal battle over the overseas rights to the *Ultraman* franchise between Tsuburaya Productions and Thai businessman Sompote Saengduenchai (founder of Chaiyo Productions).
- **The Core Dispute**: Sompote claimed a "1976 Document" granted him exclusive worldwide rights (excluding Japan) to Ultraman. Tsuburaya maintained the document was a forgery.
- **Legal Outcomes**:
  - **2008 (Thailand)**: The Supreme Court of Thailand ruled in favor of Tsuburaya, declaring the document a forgery.
  - **2018/2019 (United States)**: A U.S. Federal Court jury unanimously found the document to be a forgery, a ruling later upheld by the Ninth Circuit.
  - **2026 (Japan)**: On May 14, 2026, the Tokyo District Court fully upheld Tsuburaya Productions' claims, definitively confirming the non-existence of overseas exploitation rights under the forged 1976 Document.
- **Conclusion**: Tsuburaya Productions has solidified its status as the sole, rightful owner of the *Ultraman* IP worldwide.

## Tsuburaya Family Drama & Corporate Buyout
For over four decades after Eiji Tsuburaya's death in 1970, Tsuburaya Productions was an independent, family-run business. However, it was plagued by internal turmoil, often referred to as the "family drama."
- **Financial & Leadership Struggles**: In the 1990s and 2000s, the company faced severe financial difficulties due to over-budget productions, declining TV ratings, and intense infighting among Tsuburaya family members and executives over leadership.
- **The 2007 Sale**: The mounting debt and internal conflicts forced the family to sell the company to advertising agency TYO Inc. in October 2007, ending the family's independent control over the studio.
- **Current Ownership**: In 2010, the company shifted to its current corporate structure and is now co-owned by **Tsuburaya Fields Holdings (51%)** and **Bandai Namco Holdings (49%)**. This transition brought financial stability, improved merchandising, and ended the era of family mismanagement.

## Shin Ultraman & Hideaki Anno Drama
The 2022 film *Shin Ultraman* (written/co-produced by **Hideaki Anno**, sometimes mistakenly referred to as Hideoki Uno, and directed by Shinji Higuchi) was highly successful but surrounded by behind-the-scenes friction:
- **Creative Choices**: Anno strictly adhered to original artist Tohl Narita's vision by famously removing the "Color Timer" from Ultraman's chest. This grounded, serious reimagining drew mixed reactions from some long-time fans who held different expectations.
- **Corporate Friction & Stalled Sequels**: Anno originally envisioned a "Shin" trilogy (including *Shin Ultraseven*). However, recent reports (as of 2026) highlight internal management controversies at Tsuburaya Productions, including the departure of key executives who championed the collaboration. This corporate friction, combined with Anno's personal burnout, has effectively stalled any sequel plans.

## Chinese Ultraman (Dragon Force) Dispute
In 2017, the Chinese animation studio BlueArc Animation produced an unauthorized CGI film titled *Dragon Force: So Long, Ultraman* without any permission from Tsuburaya Productions.
- **The Justification**: BlueArc claimed they had legally licensed the character from UM Corporation/Chaiyo Productions, leveraging the fraudulent "1976 Document" forged by Sompote Sands.
- **Market & Fan Backlash**: The film was overwhelmingly despised and boycotted by Chinese fans and the global community. It was heavily mocked as a "bootleg" due to poor animation, bizarre marketing (featuring actors in body paint instead of suits), and a hideous redesign of the character (such as an elongated chin and weird proportions). Official franchise alumni, including Takeshi Tsuruno (Ultraman Dyna), publicly condemned it.
- **Legal Victory**: Tsuburaya Productions sued in China, and in December 2021, the Shanghai Intellectual Property Court ruled decisively in Tsuburaya's favor.
- **Outcome**: The Chinese court ordered BlueArc and its partners to cease all infringing activities, issue public apologies, and pay 2.5 million yuan in damages to Tsuburaya, closing a major loophole in the international exploitation of the IP.
